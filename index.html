The Cipher Calibration Cycle: Adaptive Hierarchical Encryption with Feedback Learning

Abstract

As AI agents increasingly participate in mission-critical workflows—from finance and defense to healthcare and climate response—the need for secure, semantically-aware, and multilingual communication becomes paramount. Existing agent systems provide orchestration and tool-calling capabilities but lack cryptographic safeguards, multilingual protocol integrity, and verifiable messaging, exposing vulnerabilities such as prompt injections, schema mimicry, cross-context spoofing, and multilingual ambiguities.

We propose श्रुvaan, the first cryptographically grounded, multilingual model-context protocol (MCP) for agent-to-agent communication. Unlike static ciphers like AES, RSA, or TLS, श्रुvaan integrates encryption directly into the evolving semantic layer, treating it as a learned, latent transformation conditioned on agent roles, policies, epochs, and multilingual contexts. Key innovations include a Hierarchical Keyed Protocol (HKP) for role- and context-bound encryption, a Proof-of-Protocol (PoP) for verifiable and tamper-evident messaging, and a Cipher Calibration Phase where coordinated agents continuously refine encryption based on contextual risk.

By enabling zero-reveal communication, multilingual ciphering, and tamper-evident semantics, श्रुvaan establishes a foundational leap in secure, role-aware AI agent communication. Its effectiveness will be benchmarked using metrics such as leakage entropy, mimic risk, and decode exclusivity score, ensuring robust and context-sensitive evaluation across multilingual and semantically diverse scenarios.

SoTA Review

While frameworks like OpenAI Function Calling, LangChain Agents, and Microsoft AutoGen have accelerated task orchestration, they remain fundamentally unencrypted and semantically shallow. They assume a cooperative sandbox, whereas the real world is adversarial and multilingual. Transport-layer protocols like gRPC offer channel-level security but are blind to meaning—they cannot verify agent identities, preserve intentions, or enforce roles based on the communication's content.

Furthermore, traditional End-to-End Encryption (E2EE) is not sufficient for agentic systems. This model is brittle, as a single key leak compromises the entire cipher; blind to insider threats and compromises; and frozen during a breach, as it cannot adapt without a full system reset. It secures the pipe, but not the meaning flowing through it.

श्रुvaan breaks this mold by treating encryption as a dynamic semantic contract rather than a static shell. It introduces a trust-native architecture with innovations like Hierarchical Keyed Protocols (HKP) and Proof-of-Protocol (PoP). This is the first approach to align encryption with meaning, role, and multilingual reality, enabling secure agent cognition in federated, policy-sensitive ecosystems.

The Cipher Calibration Cycle: A Living Encryption

The Cipher Calibration Cycle is a first-of-its-kind dynamic encryption framework. Unlike static cryptographic protocols, this architecture continuously recalibrates its encryption strategy based on traceable risks and mimicry signals from a team of six specialized AI agents. The result is a live, self-correcting semantic cipher that evolves through feedback to preserve both security and meaning. 🧠

Step 4: Mimicus Signal Leakage Probing

Mimicus detects semantic leakage by simulating imitation attempts from external LLMs. Each decryptor layer is evaluated using three metrics:

  • Entity Recovery ($E_i$): Measures how accurately the decrypted output recovers original field values. $$ E_i = \frac{|\mathcal{F}_i \cap \mathcal{F}_{\text{gold}}|}{|\mathcal{F}_{\text{gold}}|} $$
  • Structural Fidelity ($S_i$): Quantifies alignment with the expected schema format. $$ S_i = 1 - \frac{\text{EditDist}(T_i, T_{\text{schema}})}{\max(|T_i|, |T_{\text{schema}}|)} $$
  • Semantic Drift ($\Delta_i$): Captures deviation in semantic space. $$ \Delta_i = 1 - \cos(\phi_i, \phi_{\text{ref}}) $$

The composite leakage score at layer $i$ is given by $L_i = \alpha E_i + \beta S_i - \gamma \Delta_i$, and total leakage across all layers is $L_{\text{leak}}^{(l)} = \sum_{i=1}^{7} L_i$.

Step 5: Probator Decipherability Risk Assessment

Probator red-teams the encrypted prompt without keys, using heuristics to infer latent intent and calculate an aggregated leakage score ($L_{\text{total}}$). Based on these tests, two key vectors are generated:

  • Leakage Vector: $ \vec{\mathcal{L}} = [0.20, 0.28, 0.43, 0.11, 0.14, 0.17, 0.22] $
  • Decipherability Risk Vector: $ \vec{\mathcal{D}} = [0.15, 0.24, 0.39, 0.09, 0.12, 0.13, 0.18] $

Step 6: Gradient-Based Encryption Adjustment

The Praeceptor agent uses the risk signals from Probator and Mimicus to update the encryption parameters ($ \theta $). The composite loss is defined as:

$$ L_{\text{praeceptor}} = \beta_1 \cdot L_{\text{mimic}} + \beta_2 \cdot L_{\text{risk}} + \beta_3 \cdot L_{\text{entropy}} $$

This loss is minimized using a gradient-based update rule, which closes the semantic calibration loop and reinforces encryption modes that are resilient to adversarial inspection.

$$ \theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \cdot \nabla_\theta L_{\text{praeceptor}} $$
